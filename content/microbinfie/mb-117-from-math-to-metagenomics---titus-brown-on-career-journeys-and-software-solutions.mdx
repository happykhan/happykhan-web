# Micro Binfie Podcast Episode: Interview with Titus Brown

In this episode of the **Micro Binfie Podcast**, hosts Andrew Page and Lee Katz interview Titus Brown. They discuss Titus's educational journey from studying math and physics to becoming a bioinformatician with a focus on metagenomics and software development.

## Topics Discussed

- **Titus's Background**: 
  - Educational background in math, physics, digital evolution research, and developmental biology.
  
- **Transition to Bioinformatics**:
  - Shifted focus to bioinformatics in the 1990s to manage the influx of genomic data.
  - Early development of tools for comparative genomics and sequence analysis.
  
- **Software Philosophy**:
  - Emphasizes creating usable software with comprehensive documentation.

- **Research Areas**:
  - Work in transcriptomics, metagenomics, and k-mers while at Michigan State University.
  - Innovations in digital normalization for handling large sequencing datasets.
  
- **Projects at UC Davis**:
  - Continued work on metagenomics.
  - Developed software such as **khmer** and **sourmash**.
  
- **Data Challenges**:
  - Discussed challenges related to data reuse and accessibility in science.

## Additional summary:

- **Background and Evolution**: The podcast features discussions about transitioning from a math background to microbial bioinformatics. The speaker's journey began with digital evolution research, leading to genome sequencing and bioinformatics tool development due to data overflow in biological research.

- **Tool Development and Open Source**: The speaker developed computational tools for large-scale sequence analyses, initially focusing on BLAST searches, later moving to create customized software for comparative sequence analysis. They emphasized the importance of developing user-friendly software with comprehensive documentation and tutorials to aid researchers in conducting their experiments independently.

- **Microbial Bioinformatics Focus**: The conversation highlights the speaker's focus shift to non-model transcriptomics and metagenomics, driven by the proliferation of sequencing data. The speaker developed computational methods to manage large data sets, highlighting the significance of k-mer based approaches in bioinformatics.

- **Methodologies and Tools**:
  - **Digital Normalization**: A technique devised by the speaker to manage sequence data overload, such as highly sequenced datasets, by estimating read coverage and removing excessive data.
  - **MinHash and SourMash**: The speaker was inspired by papers on MinHash, leading to the development of SourMash, a tool applying MinHash to process k-mer datasets efficiently for genomics and metagenomics.
  - **Gambit**: A method mentioned for targeting specific k-mers, useful in microbial typing. It focuses on k-mers with specific prefixes—facilitating gene-level studies in bacteria.

- **Challenges in Microbial Bioinformatics**:
  - Handling large amounts of sequencing data requires efficient data management and tool development.
  - The importance of making data reusable and interoperable is emphasized, recognized as a significant problem in the bioinformatics field.
  - The necessity for socio-technical solutions to address data reuse challenges, encompassing both technological and policy aspects, especially across different geographic and legal landscapes.

- **Creative Insights**: The speaker notes the unpredictability of creativity in scientific careers, sharing personal views on finding innovative solutions in bioinformatics through spontaneous insights and continuous exploration.

- **Community Collaboration and Infrastructure**: Participation in data-centric infrastructure projects, like the NIH Data Commons, highlighted the need for effective collaboration and understanding of data sharing practices in the scientific community.
