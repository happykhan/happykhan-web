# Sequencing 1500 SARS-CoV-2 Genomes in Norfolk, UK

In this discussion, Justin O'Grady, Andrew Page, and Alison Mather share insights into their work on sequencing 1,500 SARS-CoV-2 genomes from the Norfolk region in the UK. Their research demonstrates the use of genomic epidemiology to provide a detailed, near real-time view of the pandemic's progression. 

## Key Points

- **Lineages Identified:** In Norfolk and surrounding areas, 100 distinct UK lineages were identified. Among these, 16 lineages found in key workers were not observed in patients or in community care.

- **Genomic Sequencing Coverage:** 
  - 172 genomes from SARS-CoV-2 positive samples were sequenced per 100,000 population, representing 42.6% of all positive cases.
  - Genomes from 1,035 cases were sequenced to high quality.

- **Comparative Sequencing Efforts:** Out of 103 countries, only 5 have sequenced more SARS-CoV-2 genomes than those analyzed for this study from Norfolk.

- **Study Period:** The samples covered the entire first wave of infections from March to August 2020.

- **Evolution and Mutations:** 
  - A stable evolutionary rate of 2 SNPs (single nucleotide polymorphisms) per month was observed.
  - The D614G mutation was identified as the dominant genotype and is associated with increased transmission.

- **Reinfection Evidence:** No evidence of reinfection was found in 42 cases with longitudinal samples.

- **Sublineage Discovery:** Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) identified a sublineage associated with care facilities and ruled out nosocomial outbreaks (hospital-acquired infections).

- **Outbreak Analysis:** Rapid WGS confirmed the relatedness of cases from an outbreak at a food processing facility.

For further details, the manuscript is available here: [MedRxiv Manuscript](https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2020.09.28.20201475v1). 

This research highlights the power of genomic sequencing in understanding and tracking the spread of SARS-CoV-2, providing valuable insights for public health responses.
